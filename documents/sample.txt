
Albert Einstein was born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg in the German Empire.
He developed the theory of relativity, one of the two pillars of modern physics.
His mass–energy equivalence formula E = mc² has been called the world's most famous equation.
He received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect.
Einstein moved to the United States in 1933 when Hitler came to power in Germany.
He worked at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey until his death in 1955.
Einstein published more than 300 scientific papers and more than 150 non-scientific works.
He collaborated with many physicists including Niels Bohr, Max Planck, and Werner Heisenberg.
Einstein's general theory of relativity predicted the bending of light around massive objects.
In 1905, Einstein published four groundbreaking papers, a period called his "Annus Mirabilis" or miracle year.
Einstein was married twice — first to Mileva Marić in 1903, and then to his cousin Elsa Löwenthal in 1919.
He was a strong advocate for civil rights and was offered the presidency of Israel in 1952, which he declined.
Einstein died on April 18, 1955, in Princeton, New Jersey, at the age of 76.
His brain was preserved after death and has been the subject of several studies.
Einstein played the violin and had a deep love for music, particularly the works of Mozart and Bach.
The photoelectric effect explained how light can eject electrons from a metal surface.
Einstein's special theory of relativity introduced the concept that space and time are interwoven into spacetime.
Gravitational waves, predicted by Einstein's general relativity, were first directly detected in 2015 by LIGO.
Einstein was a pacifist but signed a letter to President Roosevelt urging research into nuclear weapons, fearing Nazi Germany would develop them first.
The Einstein ring is an optical phenomenon caused by the gravitational lensing of light from a distant source.
